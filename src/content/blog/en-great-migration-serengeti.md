The Great Serengeti Migration is considered one of the most spectacular natural events on the planet. Each year, over 1.5 million wildebeest, along with hundreds of thousands of zebras and gazelles, travel a circuit of approximately 800 kilometers through the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem in search of fresh grazing and water.

## What Is the Great Migration?

The Great Migration is not a single event but a continuous cycle that repeats every year, driven by rainfall and grass availability. The herds follow a circular pattern through the Serengeti plains in Tanzania and the Masai Mara National Reserve in Kenya.

The phenomenon primarily involves three species:
- **Blue wildebeest**: ~1.5 million individuals
- **Plains zebra**: ~200,000 individuals
- **Thomson's gazelle**: ~300,000 individuals

## The Annual Migration Cycle

### January — February: Calving Season
Herds concentrate on the southern Serengeti short-grass plains, particularly the Ndutu area. Over 500,000 wildebeest calves are born within a few weeks.

### March — May: Westward Movement
With the onset of the long rains, herds begin migrating west and north through the Serengeti, following fresh grass growth.

### June — July: Grumeti River Crossings
Herds reach the Western Corridor and must cross the Grumeti River, where Nile crocodiles up to 5 meters long wait.

### August — October: Mara River Crossings
The most iconic moment of the migration. Herds build up on the banks of the Mara River, sometimes for days, before plunging en masse into the crossing.

### November — December: The Return South
Short rains signal the return. Herds descend through eastern Serengeti back toward the southern plains, completing the cycle.

## Where to See the Migration

The ideal location depends on the time of year:
- **Southern Serengeti (Ndutu)**: December to March
- **Western Corridor (Grumeti)**: May to July
- **Northern Serengeti (Kogatende)**: July to October
- **Eastern Serengeti**: November to December
